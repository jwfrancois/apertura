From 1826 to Today

History of
Photography

From the earliest experiments with light-sensitive chemicals to sophisticated digital imaging systems, photography has evolved through countless innovations in optical engineering, chemical processes, and electronic technology.

Invention · Movement · Technology · Key Figure

Two Centuries
of the Medium

A working chronology — not every year that mattered, but the years that changed what a photograph could be.

5th c. BCE

Camera Obscura Principles

Chinese philosopher Mozi records earliest understanding of camera obscura principles. Aristotle provides mathematical analysis around 330 BCE.

1727

Photochemical Discovery

Johann Heinrich Schulze proves silver salts darken by light exposure, establishing the photochemical foundation for all photography.

1826-27

First Permanent Photograph

Nicephore Niepce creates "View from the Window at Le Gras," the earliest surviving photograph from nature, requiring 8+ hours exposure.

1839

The Daguerreotype

Louis Daguerre publicly introduces the daguerreotype process, producing highly detailed permanent photographs on silver-plated copper.

1841

Calotype Process

William Henry Fox Talbot patents the calotype, establishing the negative-positive process that would dominate photography for 150 years.

1861

Color Photography Demonstrated

James Clerk Maxwell and Thomas Sutton demonstrate color photography using the three-color method still fundamental to color imaging today.

1885-1915

Pictorialism Movement

Photographers manipulate images to establish photography as fine art. The Photo-Secession founded by Stieglitz and Steichen in 1902.

1888

Kodak Revolution

George Eastman introduces the Kodak Number 1 box camera with "You press the button, we do the rest," making photography accessible to amateurs.

1890

Documentary Photography Emerges

Jacob Riis publishes "How the Other Half Lives," using photography for social reform and establishing documentary photography as a tool for change.

1925

The 35mm Leica

The Leica camera brings 35mm format to still photography, establishing the compact system that would dominate professional photography for the next 70 years.

1932-35

Group f/64 Founded

Ansel Adams, Edward Weston, and others form Group f/64, advocating "straight photography" with maximum sharpness and clarity.

1935

Kodachrome Film

Kodachrome multi-layered color reversal film introduced, setting new standards for color photography quality and stability for decades to come.

1947

Magnum Photos Founded

Robert Capa co-founds Magnum Photos cooperative, establishing a model for photographer-owned agencies that remains influential to this day.

1952

The Decisive Moment

Henri Cartier-Bresson publishes "The Decisive Moment," one of the most influential photography texts ever published — and a manifesto for street photography.

1975

First Digital Camera

Steven Sasson at Kodak invents the first digital camera prototype, capturing 0.01 megapixel images onto digital cassette tape — the future, hidden inside a lab.

1991

First Commercial DSLR

The Kodak DCS-100 becomes the first commercially available digital SLR camera, combining a Nikon F3 body with digital sensor technology.

2000

Camera Phone Era

The J-SH04 becomes the first commercially available camera phone, beginning the transformation of photography into ubiquitous daily practice.

2024-25

AI Integration

AI-powered tools reshape professional workflows: automated culling, smart editing, and computational photography become standard practice across the industry.

Influential Photographers

Key Figures Who
Shaped the Medium

Four photographers whose choices about what to point the camera at, and how, defined the practice for everyone who came after.

A

Alfred Stieglitz

1864 — 1946

Championed photography as art, founded Photo-Secession and Camera Work journal — the magazine that argued, in print, that photographs could be art.

A

Ansel Adams

1902 — 1984

Pioneer of nature photography, developed the Zone System for exposure control. Brought the American West into the world's imagination, in monochrome.

H

Henri Cartier-Bresson

1908 — 2004

Defined "the decisive moment," co-founded Magnum Photos. Made the small camera into a serious instrument, and the street into a serious subject.

C

Cindy Sherman

1954 —

Conceptual photographer exploring identity, gender, and media representation. The subject and the photographer are often the same person — and that is the work.

1885 — 1915

Pictorialism

Photographers manipulated images to express creativity, tell stories, and create dream-like imagery that elevated the medium above mere documentation.

The Photo-Secession, founded by Alfred Stieglitz and Edward Steichen in 1902, aimed to compel recognition of photography as a distinct medium of individual artistic expression. Camera Work journal featured high-quality photogravure images establishing Pictorialist aesthetics as serious art.

1932 — 1935

Group f/64

A direct reaction against Pictorialism, advocating for photography that embraced the medium's mechanical characteristics rather than imitating painting.

Founded by Ansel Adams, Edward Weston, Imogen Cunningham, and others in San Francisco. Named after the smallest aperture setting, symbolizing commitment to maximum depth of field and overall sharpness. Their principles became foundational to much subsequent photographic practice.

The Legacy

Photography's Impact
on Society

From Jacob Riis's documentation of New York slums that spurred reform legislation to photojournalistic images that shaped public understanding of conflicts, photography's apparent objectivity has been leveraged for advocacy, propaganda, and documentation across the modern era.

As photography enters its third century, fundamental principles established by early pioneers remain relevant even as technologies continue to transform practice — suggesting that photography's essential appeal will continue driving innovation into an uncertain but promising future.
