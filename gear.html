Start with what you have

Modern phones (2023 or newer) take genuinely excellent photos in good light. If you are at Level 1 of the curriculum, the most expensive thing you can do is skip the camera purchase and spend your first six months learning composition and light with the camera you already carry every day. When you outgrow the phone — when you find yourself wanting shallow depth of field, optical zoom, or low-light performance that a phone physically cannot deliver — that is the right time to buy a real camera.

Tier 1 · Beginner

Your first interchangeable-lens camera

$500 – $900 with kit lens

You have completed the Foundation level. You understand exposure, focus, and white balance. You want a real camera with a larger sensor than a phone, manual controls, and the ability to swap lenses. Do not spend more than $900 on a body at this stage. The skill ceiling of a $900 body is well above your current skill ceiling — save the rest for lenses, which you will keep for years; camera bodies change every 3-5 years.

What to buy with the body

Skip the manufacturer kit zoom for the second lens. Use the kit zoom for 30 days, then buy one fast prime (nifty fifty). On a phone, you cannot blur the background. On a camera, a 50mm f/1.8 is the cheapest way to learn what shallow depth of field actually feels like.

  • First add-on lens: A "nifty fifty" — the 50mm f/1.8 in your mount. Usually $100-$200 used. Teaches you everything about depth of field.
  • Memory card: One fast UHS-II SD card (64GB minimum). Skip the manufacturer's bundled card if it is slow.
  • Skip for now: Tripod, flash, filters, extra batteries (you can add one battery in month 3 if you need it).

Tier 2 · Intermediate

Your first full-frame (or high-end APS-C) camera

$1,200 – $1,800 with kit lens

You have completed the Intermediate level. You have shot 5,000+ frames. You know what you like to photograph. You want shallower depth of field, better low-light performance, and the confidence to shoot at ISO 6400 without worry. This is the sweet spot for most serious hobbyists — full-frame sensors deliver visibly better images than phones or entry-level APS-C in any condition that is not bright daylight.

Tier 3 · Advanced

High-end full-frame for serious work

$1,900 – $3,000 body only

You need reliable autofocus that just works, dual card slots, weather sealing, and a body that will not let you down on a paid shoot. These are the mid-range full-frame bodies that competing reviews call "the best balance" in 2025–2026.

LENS ECOSYSTEMS

The mount matters more than the body

You are not buying a camera. You are buying into a lens mount — the system of lenses you will use for the next decade. Bodies change every 3-5 years. Lenses change every 10-20. Choose the mount you want to grow into, then pick the body that gets you into it affordably.

Three rules of thumb for first lenses after your kit zoom:

  • Buy the 50mm f/1.8 first. Cheap, sharp, teaches you depth of field. Every system has one. ($100-$300)
  • Then the 24-70mm f/2.8 (or f/4 if budget is tight). The professional standard zoom. Use it for a year before adding anything else. ($500-$2,300)
  • Then a specialty prime — 35mm, 85mm, or 16-35mm depending on what you actually shoot. By this point you will know.

DECISION GUIDE

Which one is right for you?

Answer the questions below honestly. The right camera for a 16-year-old who just discovered street photography is not the right camera for a 45-year-old wedding photographer.

Q1 · What kind of photography do you actually do?

If "I don't know yet"

You are at Level 1 of the curriculum. The right answer is the cheapest body that gets you the standard kit zoom and a 50mm f/1.8. Fujifilm X-T30 III or Sony α6100 (used). Do not spend more than $900.

→ Tier 1 picks

Q2 · Do you shoot more video than stills?

If yes

Skip APS-C entirely. The Sony α7 IV / α7C II give you the strongest hybrid video ecosystem. The Canon R5 II is the most powerful hybrid body in 2025 for video specifically. For pure video-first work, consider the Panasonic S5 II or Nikon Z6 III for the best video specs under $2,500.

→ Sony α7C II, Canon R5 II, Nikon Z6 III

Q3 · Do you want to keep this system for 10+ years?

Pick the mount, then the body

You are not married to a single body, but the lenses you buy today should still work on tomorrow's flagship. Sony E-mount has the largest, most diverse lens selection including best third-party support. Canon RF is closed to third parties but has excellent native L glass. Nikon Z is the sweet spot for glass quality.

→ Start with the system, not the body

Q4 · Budget reality check

Buy the body, then lenses, then maybe more body

A $2,000 camera with a $300 lens takes worse photos than a $1,500 camera with an $800 lens. Allocate at least 50% of your budget to glass. A photographer with one great lens and a used body will always outperform a photographer with a flagship body and the kit zoom.

→ Lenses outlast bodies by 3-5x

Q5 · Are you already invested in a system?

Stay in your mount

If you have Canon EF, Nikon F, or Sony A-mount lenses, look at the adapter path. Sony E accepts most third-party lenses via cheap adapters. Nikon Z and Canon RF have first-party adapters that preserve full autofocus on many older lenses. Do not switch systems just because of one review.

→ Adapter + familiar glass > new system + new glass

Q6 · Is the reviewer's favorite camera the right one for you?

Probably not

Every review has a winner that fits the reviewer's hand, eye, and genre. Rent before you buy if you can. Lens Prototypes and MPB both rent cameras. If a body feels wrong in the hand after 30 minutes, it will feel wrong after 30 months.

→ Rent first, then buy

WHAT TO AVOID

Common mistakes when buying your first camera

Photography communities have seen every mistake. The list below is the short version of "what I wish someone had told me before I spent $3,000."

✕

Buying a flagship body to "grow into"

You will not grow into a $6,000 body. You will use it for six months, never touch 90% of the features, and feel guilty every time you leave it in the bag. Buy a body you can use 100% of today. Bodies depreciate. Lenses appreciate.

✕

Buying a "kit" without a kit lens plan

The kit zoom is fine for 30 days, then it sits in the drawer. Plan from day one to add one fast prime. The 50mm f/1.8 (or its equivalent) is the highest ROI purchase in photography.

✕

Switching systems to follow a YouTube review

Selling two lenses to afford one new body is almost always a bad trade. The lenses you already own are worth more to you than the new body's marginal improvements. Watch the review, then keep what you have.

✕

Spending on accessories before lenses

Tripods, filters, bags, lens hoods, extra batteries — these are real costs, but they are the last 5% of the kit. The lens is the first 80% of the image. Buy glass first.

✕

Trusting a "best of 2025" list as a buying guide

These are written by reviewers with different shooting styles, different hands, and different needs. Use them as a shortlist, then verify with reviews from photographers who shoot what you shoot.

✕

Buying more megapixels than you need

24MP is enough for an A2 print at 300 DPI. 33MP is enough for A1. 45MP is enough for poster-sized. More megapixels mean larger files, slower workflows, and more storage — without making most photographs visibly better.

✕

Skipping the "boring" kit lens for a fast prime

Counter-intuitive but true: you need the kit zoom to learn focal length. A 50mm prime teaches you depth of field, not composition. Shoot 1,000 frames with the zoom, then choose your specialty prime.
